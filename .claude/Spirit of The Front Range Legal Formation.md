# **PART I: LEGAL BRIEFING AND STRATEGIC APPROACH**

## **Opening Statement: Grounding in Place**

SPIRIT of the Front Range emerges from a fundamental recognition: the eastern slope of the Colorado Rockies, from the Wyoming border through the Palmer Divide and into the Arkansas River watershed, constitutes a coherent bioregional system that shapes the lives of over four million residents. This landscape—defined by the meeting of mountain and plain, the flow of water from snowpack to aquifer, and the ecological corridors that connect alpine tundra to shortgrass prairie—has no institutional representation adequate to its scale and significance.

The cities of Boulder, Denver, Fort Collins, Colorado Springs, and their surrounding communities share watershed systems, air quality challenges, wildlife migration patterns, and climate vulnerabilities that transcend municipal and county boundaries. Yet existing governance structures—optimized for political jurisdictions rather than ecological realities—leave communities without effective coordination mechanisms for bioregional stewardship.

SPIRIT of the Front Range is designed as a *backbone institution*—an organizational form that provides necessary legal infrastructure for bioregional coordination while explicitly recentering authority in and through the bioregion itself – its people, communities, and ecologies. This approach recognizes that bioregional identity is fundamentally a *commons* that cannot be enclosed by any single organization. The nonprofit corporation serves as an interface with legacy legal and financial systems, enabling resource flows and regulatory compliance, while a parallel network structure (to be developed through community processes) embodies the principles of distributed, place-based coordination.

## **Strategic Legal Framework**

The legal structure proposed here implements a **phased approach** that balances immediate operational needs with long-term aspirations for democratic innovation:

**Phase 1 (Current): Standard Nonprofit Foundation**

* Colorado nonprofit corporation with 501(c)(3) tax-exempt status  
* Board-governed structure with built-in accountability mechanisms  
* Credibly neutral grant distribution through established protocols (e.g., quadratic funding and community-advised allocation of resources)  
* Legal capacity to receive donations, grants, and fiscal sponsorship arrangements

**Phase 2 (12-30 months): Network Integration**

* Development of formal membership structures linking nonprofit to bioregional network  
* Implementation of network advisory councils with increasing governance authority  
* Bylaw amendments enabling community-nominated board positions

**Phase 3 (30-60 months): Full Dual-Structure Implementation**

* Formalized accountability relationships between nonprofit and bioregional DAO/network  
* Network veto power over major institutional decisions  
* Potential subsidiary structures or affiliated entities as needed

The bylaws below include **placeholder provisions** for Phase 2 and 3 developments, marked as \[RESERVED FOR FUTURE AMENDMENT\]. This approach provides a clear pathway for evolution while maintaining legal stability during the foundation period.

# **PART II: IRS DESIGNATION OPTIMIZATION**

## **Recommended Primary Designation: 501(c)(3) Public Charity**

Based on SPIRIT of the Front Range's mission and planned activities, we recommend seeking recognition as a **501(c)(3) public charity** classified under **Section 509(a)(1)** of the Internal Revenue Code, qualifying as an organization described in Section 170(b)(1)(A)(vi)—an organization that receives a substantial part of its support from governmental units and/or the general public.

### **Qualifying Exempt Purposes**

SPIRIT of the Front Range's activities qualify under multiple 501(c)(3) exempt purposes:

| Exempt Purpose | Application to SOTFR Activities |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Charitable** | Grant-making to community projects, environmental remediation, community development, and lessening neighborhood tensions |
| **Educational** | Bioregional education, ecological literacy programs, capacity building for community organizations, research and publication |
| **Scientific** | Citizen science initiatives, ecological monitoring, watershed research, climate adaptation studies |
| **Environmental** | Combating environmental deterioration, conservation activities, ecological restoration coordination |

### **Public Charity vs. Private Foundation Analysis**

Public charity status (vs. private foundation) is strongly preferred because it:

* Allows donors to deduct contributions up to 60% of AGI (vs. 30% for private foundations)  
* Avoids the 1.39% excise tax on net investment income  
* Faces fewer restrictions on lobbying and programmatic activities  
* Is not subject to private foundation self-dealing and minimum distribution rules  
* Provides greater credibility with government and foundation funders

SOTFR should qualify for public charity status through the **public support test**, receiving more than one-third of its support from public sources (government grants, foundation grants, and contributions from many individuals). The diverse funding model—combining foundation grants, individual donations, and earned revenue from fiscal sponsorship fees—supports this classification.

### **Fiscal Sponsorship Capacity**

The 501(c)(3) designation enables SOTFR to serve as a fiscal sponsor for emerging bioregional projects that lack their own tax-exempt status. This is a core function of the backbone institution model. Two fiscal sponsorship models are recommended:

**Model A (Comprehensive):** Projects operate as programs of SOTFR, with full legal and financial responsibility held by the nonprofit. Appropriate for early-stage initiatives requiring significant infrastructure support.

**Model BC (Pre-Approved Grant):** SOTFR receives grants earmarked for independent projects, then re-grants to qualifying organizations upon verification of exempt purposes. Appropriate for more established projects seeking temporary fiscal sponsorship.

### **Considerations for Future Affiliate Structures**

As the organization evolves, additional legal structures may be warranted:

* **501(c)(4) Social Welfare Organization:** If substantial lobbying or electoral engagement becomes a priority, a separate (c)(4) could conduct activities that would jeopardize (c)(3) status. Donations are not tax-deductible.  
* **LLC (Program-Related Investment Vehicle):** For activities generating earned revenue or holding real property, an LLC owned by the nonprofit preserves liability protection while maintaining tax-exempt pass-through treatment.  
* **Cooperative or Mutual Benefit Corporation:** For future network-governed structures requiring membership voting rights that exceed nonprofit board governance capacity.

**ARTICLE I — NAME, OFFICES, AND MISSION FRAMEWORK**

**Section 1.1. Name.** The name of this corporation is SPIRIT of the Front Range (the "Corporation").

**Section 1.2. Principal Office.** The principal office of the Corporation shall be located at such place within the State of Colorado as the Board of Directors may from time to time designate.

**Section 1.3. Other Offices.** The Corporation may have such other offices, either within or without the State of Colorado, as the Board of Directors may designate or as the business of the Corporation may require from time to time.

**Section 1.4. Bioregional Scope.** The Corporation's geographic focus is the Front Range bioregion of Colorado, generally understood to encompass the eastern slope of the Rocky Mountains from the Wyoming border to the Palmer Divide, including the metropolitan areas of Denver, Boulder, Fort Collins, and Colorado Springs, and extending to the ecological boundaries of the associated watersheds, ecosystems, and communities. This geographic scope is understood as an ecological and cultural reference rather than a rigid jurisdictional boundary.

**Section 1.5. Institutional Acknowledgment.** The Corporation acknowledges that the Front Range bioregion is a commons that cannot be enclosed or controlled by any single institution, including this Corporation. The Corporation exists to serve and facilitate bioregional coordination, not to claim authority over the bioregion itself. This acknowledgment shall guide the interpretation of all provisions of these Bylaws and the conduct of all Corporation activities.

**ARTICLE II — PURPOSES AND POWERS**

**Section 2.1. Purposes.** The Corporation is organized and shall be operated exclusively for charitable, educational, and scientific purposes as set forth in the Articles of Incorporation.

**Section 2.2. Core Functions.** In furtherance of its purposes, the Corporation shall serve as a bioregional backbone institution with the following core functions:

1) **Resource Translation:** Converting philanthropic and governmental funding into mechanisms that support bioregional coordination activities, including but not limited to grant-making, fiscal sponsorship, and infrastructure provision;  
2) **Capacity Building:** Providing technical assistance, training, and educational resources to community organizations, civic initiatives, and individual practitioners engaged in bioregional stewardship and regeneration;  
3) **Coordination Infrastructure:** Developing and maintaining technological platforms, communication networks, and shared resources that enable distributed collaboration among bioregional denizens;  
4) **Strategic Communication:** Articulating bioregional coordination activities and outcomes to institutional denizens, funders, and the general public;  
5) **Research and Documentation:** Supporting and conducting research that advances understanding of bioregional systems, coordination mechanisms, and ecological stewardship and regeneration practices.

**Section 2.3. Powers.** The Corporation shall have all powers granted to nonprofit corporations under the Colorado Revised Nonprofit Corporation Act, subject to the limitations set forth in the Articles of Incorporation.

**ARTICLE III — BOARD OF DIRECTORS**

**Section 3.1. General Powers.** The business and affairs of the Corporation shall be managed by its Board of Directors (the "Board"). The Board may exercise all powers of the Corporation except as otherwise provided by law, the Articles of Incorporation, or these Bylaws.

**Section 3.2. Number and Composition.** The Board shall consist of not fewer than three (3) nor more than fifteen (15) directors, with the exact number to be fixed by resolution of the Board. The Board shall endeavor to maintain a composition that reflects the geographic, cultural, and ecological diversity of the Front Range bioregion.

**Section 3.3. Qualifications.** Directors shall be natural or ecological persons who have attained the age of eighteen (18) years and who demonstrate commitment to the Corporation's mission and the principles of bioregional coordination. Directors shall be residents of the Front Range bioregion and have substantial connection to and knowledge of the cultural and ecological terrain.

**Section 3.4. Election and Term.** Directors shall be elected by the Board at the annual meeting or at any regular or special meeting. Each director shall serve for a term of three (3) years and until a successor has been elected and qualified, or until earlier resignation, removal, or death.

**Section 3.5. Term Limits.** No director shall serve more than three (3) consecutive full terms. After serving three consecutive terms, a director must wait at least one (1) year before being eligible for re-election. Partial terms of less than two (2) years shall not count toward this limit.

**Section 3.6. Staggered Terms.** The directors shall be divided into three (3) classes, as nearly equal in number as possible. The term of office of directors in the first class shall expire at the first annual meeting after their election, the term of office of directors in the second class shall expire at the second annual meeting after their election, and the term of office of directors in the third class shall expire at the third annual meeting after their election. At each annual meeting thereafter, directors shall be elected for three-year terms to succeed those whose terms expire.

**Section 3.7. Vacancies.** Any vacancy occurring in the Board, including any vacancy created by an increase in the number of directors, may be filled by the affirmative vote of a majority of the remaining directors, even if less than a quorum. A director elected to fill a vacancy shall serve for the unexpired term of the predecessor in office.

**Section 3.8. Resignation.** Any director may resign at any time by giving written notice to the Chair of the Board or the Secretary. Such resignation shall take effect at the time specified therein or, if no time is specified, upon receipt of the notice by the Chair or Secretary.

**Section 3.9. Removal.** Any director may be removed from office with or without cause by the affirmative vote of two-thirds (2/3) of the directors then in office at any regular or special meeting of the Board, provided that written notice of the proposed removal has been given to all directors at least fourteen (14) days prior to the meeting.

**Section 3.10. Community-Nominated Directors.** \[RESERVED FOR FUTURE AMENDMENT\] As the Corporation develops relationships with bioregional network structures, the Board may by amendment establish one or more board positions nominated by community bodies, subject to confirmation by the Board. Such provisions shall include criteria for qualifying community bodies, nomination processes, and accountability mechanisms.

**ARTICLE IV — MEETINGS OF THE BOARD**

**Section 4.1. Annual Meeting.** The Board shall hold an annual meeting for the purpose of electing officers, receiving reports on the Corporation's activities, and transacting such other business as may properly come before the meeting. The annual meeting shall be held at such time and place as the Board may determine.

**Section 4.2. Regular Meetings.** The Board shall hold regular meetings at least quarterly at such times and places as the Board may determine. Regular meetings may be held without notice if the time and place are fixed by the Board.

**Section 4.3. Special Meetings.** Special meetings of the Board may be called by the Chair of the Board, the Executive Director (if any), or upon the written request of at least one-third (1/3) of the directors then in office.

**Section 4.4. Notice.** Notice of any special meeting shall be given at least seven (7) days prior thereto by written notice delivered personally, sent by mail, or sent by electronic transmission to each director at their address as shown on the records of the Corporation. The notice shall state the time, place, and purpose of the meeting.

**Section 4.5. Waiver of Notice.** Any director may waive notice of any meeting before, at, or after the meeting. The waiver shall be in writing, signed by the director entitled to notice, and filed with the minutes of the meeting. Attendance at a meeting shall constitute waiver of notice, except where the director attends for the express purpose of objecting to the transaction of business because the meeting is not lawfully convened.

**Section 4.6. Quorum.** A majority of the directors then in office shall constitute a quorum for the transaction of business at any meeting of the Board.

**Section 4.7. Manner of Acting.** Except as otherwise provided in these Bylaws or by law, the act of a majority of the directors present at a meeting at which a quorum is present shall be the act of the Board.

**Section 4.8. Remote Participation.** Directors may participate in a meeting of the Board by means of conference telephone, video conference, or similar communications equipment by which all persons participating in the meeting can hear each other at the same time. Participation by such means shall constitute presence in person at the meeting.

**Section 4.9. Action Without Meeting.** Any action required or permitted to be taken at a meeting of the Board may be taken without a meeting if consent in writing, setting forth the action so taken, is signed by all of the directors. Such consent shall have the same force and effect as a unanimous vote at a meeting duly held.

**ARTICLE V — OFFICERS**

**Section 5.1. Officers.** The officers of the Corporation shall be a Chair of the Board, a Vice Chair, a Secretary, and a Treasurer. The Board may elect such other officers as it deems necessary. Any two or more offices may be held by the same person, except that the Chair may not also serve as Secretary.

**Section 5.2. Election and Term.** The officers shall be elected by the Board at the annual meeting and shall serve for a term of one (1) year and until their successors have been elected and qualified.

**Section 5.3. Officer Term Limits.** No officer shall serve in the same office for more than four (4) consecutive one-year terms. After serving four consecutive terms in an office, an officer must wait at least one (1) year before being eligible for re-election to that same office.

**Section 5.4. Chair of the Board.** The Chair shall preside at all meetings of the Board, shall have general supervision of the affairs of the Corporation, and shall perform all duties incident to the office or as assigned by the Board.

**Section 5.5. Vice Chair.** The Vice Chair shall, in the absence or disability of the Chair, perform the duties and exercise the powers of the Chair. The Vice Chair shall also perform such other duties as may be assigned by the Board or the Chair.

**Section 5.6. Secretary.** The Secretary shall keep accurate records of the proceedings of all meetings of the Board, give all required notices, be custodian of the corporate records and seal, and perform all duties incident to the office or as assigned by the Board.

**Section 5.7. Treasurer.** The Treasurer shall have oversight of the financial affairs of the Corporation, ensure that accurate financial records are maintained, report on the financial condition of the Corporation at each regular Board meeting, and perform all duties incident to the office or as assigned by the Board.

**Section 5.8. Removal.** Any officer may be removed by the Board at any time, with or without cause, by the affirmative vote of a majority of the directors then in office.

**Section 5.9. Vacancies.** A vacancy in any office may be filled by the Board for the unexpired portion of the term.

**ARTICLE VI — EXECUTIVE DIRECTOR AND STAFF**

**Section 6.2. Duties.** The Executive Director shall implement the policies and directives of the Board, supervise all employees, manage the Corporation's programs and operations, and perform such other duties as may be assigned by the Board.

**ARTICLE VIII — FINANCIAL ADMINISTRATION AND TRANSPARENCY**

**Section 8.1. Fiscal Year.** The fiscal year of the Corporation shall be the calendar year unless otherwise determined by the Board.

**Section 8.2. Budget.** The Board shall adopt an annual budget prior to the beginning of each fiscal year. The budget shall allocate resources in accordance with the Corporation's mission and priorities.

**Section 8.3. Administrative Expense Cap.** The Corporation's administrative and operational expenses (excluding direct program expenses and grant distributions) shall not exceed twenty-five percent (25%) of total annual expenditures without prior approval by a two-thirds (2/3) vote of the Board. The Board shall review this cap annually and may adjust it as circumstances warrant, with any adjustment documented in meeting minutes along with the rationale for the change.

**Section 8.4. Resource Flow Transparency.** The Corporation shall maintain and publish, at least annually, a comprehensive accounting of all financial flows, including sources of revenue, administrative costs, program expenses, and distributions to grantees and fiscally sponsored projects. This accounting shall be made available to the public through the Corporation's website or other accessible means.

**Section 8.5. Grant-Making Principles.** The Corporation's grant-making activities shall be guided by the following principles:

9) Preference for credibly neutral allocation mechanisms that optimize for broad community support rather than donor preference or institutional discretion;  
10) Transparency in all grant-making criteria, processes, and decisions;  
11) Support for projects that advance bioregional coordination and revitalization across multiple forms of capital (financial, social, ecological, cultural, etc.);  
12) Accountability to the communities and ecosystems the Corporation serves.

**Section 8.6. Financial Review.** The Corporation shall have its financial statements reviewed or audited annually by an independent certified public accountant. The Board shall determine whether a review or audit is appropriate based on the Corporation's size and complexity.

**Section 8.7. Signing Authority.** All checks, drafts, or orders for the payment of money shall be signed by such officer or officers or agent or agents as the Board may designate. Contracts and other documents requiring the Corporation's signature shall be signed by the Chair, the Executive Director, or such other persons as the Board may authorize.

**ARTICLE IX — CONFLICT OF INTEREST**

**Section 9.1. Purpose.** The purpose of this conflict of interest policy is to protect the Corporation's interest when it is contemplating entering into a transaction or arrangement that might benefit the private interest of a director, officer, or key employee of the Corporation.

**Section 9.2. Definition of Conflict.** A conflict of interest exists when a director, officer, or key employee has a direct or indirect financial interest in a transaction or arrangement with the Corporation, or when such person has a relationship with another organization that has a financial interest in a transaction with the Corporation.

**Section 9.3. Duty to Disclose.** Any director, officer, or key employee who has an actual or potential conflict of interest must disclose the existence and nature of the conflict to the Board or appropriate committee before any discussion or action is taken on the matter.

**Section 9.4. Procedures.** Upon disclosure of a conflict:

13) The interested person shall not participate in discussion of, or vote on, the matter giving rise to the conflict;  
14) The disinterested directors shall determine whether a more advantageous transaction is reasonably attainable;  
15) If the disinterested directors determine that the transaction is in the Corporation's best interest and is fair and reasonable, they may approve the transaction by a majority vote.

**Section 9.5. Documentation.** The minutes of any meeting at which a conflict is disclosed and acted upon shall record the nature of the conflict, the interested person's disclosure, any discussion, and the vote of the disinterested directors.

**Section 9.6. Annual Disclosure.** Each director, officer, and key employee shall annually complete and sign a conflict of interest disclosure statement identifying any actual or potential conflicts.
