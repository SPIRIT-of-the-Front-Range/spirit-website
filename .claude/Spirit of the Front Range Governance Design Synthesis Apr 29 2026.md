## Executive Summary

On April 29, 2026, the Spirit of the Front Range steward team held two back-to-back working sessions that resolved the core architecture of how this bioregional initiative will govern itself, fund itself, and relate to the broader community it is incubating. After months of conversation across constitutional drafting, DUNA exploration, board formation, and prior workshops, the outline of Spirit's governance form became unambiguous in this room.

**The decisions that emerged:**

1. **Spirit will operate a tiered governance structure** — Board → Stewardship Council → Circle Leads → Project Leads — applying sociocratic principles inside a 501(c)(3) shell. The Stewardship Council is the operational heart; the board's role is reduced to a semi-perfunctory legal-compliance function.  
     
2. **Five operating circles were confirmed** (Admin/Tech & Systems, Culture/Community/Conflict, Story/Media/Campaign, Programming/Education/Events, Finance/Fundraising), each led by a circle lead who sits on the Stewardship Council.  
     
3. **The Front Range Commons is a parallel, distinct entity** that Spirit feeds and protects but does not control. **Commoners elect a Wisdom Council**, which in turn **appoints Common Stewards** who run the day-to-day operations of the Commons. The Wisdom Council functions as the Commons' equivalent of a board.  
     
4. **Spirit is reframed as a "lattice"** — the minimum institutional infrastructure required to make Commons activity possible.  
     
5. **A funding cascade was agreed**: the first funds raised goes to Spirit's ops and salaries; the next tranche funds Common Steward stipends; everything beyond that flows into a commons grant pool, with at least 50% of total raise targeted at programmatic disbursement to keep Spirit safely below charity watchdog overhead thresholds (administrative \<35%).  
     
6. **Decision-making protocols** were sketched: appointments (not elections) for circle leads and project leads inside Spirit; consent-based decisions inside the Stewardship Council; quadratic voting for Commons grant allocation; lazy consent for outbound public communications.

Why it matters: this is the first time the team has articulated the relationship between the legal vehicle (Spirit, a 501(c)(3) operating sociocratically), the social body (the Commons, self-organizing and bioregional), and the financial flow between them as a single coherent system. The clarity that emerged here is sufficient to draft an operating agreement, brief incoming circle leads, present at Friday's community gathering, and seed the broader onboarding meeting on May 16\.

This document is a *living spec.* It captures the reasoning behind each decision so that future iterations — slide decks, fundraising narratives, formal agreements — can preserve the underlying logic, not just the surface form.

## 1\. Organizational Architecture

### **The Tiered Structure**

Spirit's governance is organized as four nested tiers, each with distinct authority and scope:

```
                       ┌───────────────────┐
                       │       BOARD       │  ← legal compliance only
                       └─────────┬─────────┘
                                 │ appoints
                                 ▼
                  ┌────────────────────────────┐
                  │    STEWARDSHIP COUNCIL     │  ← strategy + vision
                  │  (Stewards + Circle Leads) │     operations
                  └─────────────┬──────────────┘
                                │ appoints
                                ▼
                       ┌───────────────────┐
                       │   CIRCLE LEADS    │  ← circle-scope decisions
                       └─────────┬─────────┘
                                 │ appoints
                                 ▼
                       ┌───────────────────┐
                       │   PROJECT LEADS   │  ← project-scope decisions
                       └───────────────────┘
```

### **The Board**

The board is the legally required nonprofit oversight body. By design, it is *not* where the work happens.

"I think the stewardship council is operational. It's kind of like the mother circle of spirit's operations. And the board is like the legally required oversight committee that just checks in on a quarterly basis with the stewards to be like, *y'all good*. And it's mostly perfunctory and performative. But we do it." — Benjamin (\~21:36)

"Yeah, we're doing this a little bit non-traditionally. And so we're applying kind of sociocracy principles to running a nonprofit." — Benjamin (\~21:10)

The board appoints the stewards. When the board convenes, the stewards are present, because some stewards will also be board members, but the operational reality of the org lives at the stewardship council level.

"I think the stewards are on the board... The board appoints the stewards. And then the stewards appoint the circle leads." — group consensus (\~21:12)

**Legal posture:** the working agreements that govern Spirit's day-to-day operation are *parallel* to the formal bylaws. The bylaws will be designed to permit (not constrain) this sociocratic operation.

"TLDR, I would say consider this a parallel structure that we honor through our agreements... my goal is to kind of cover our asses legally, where on paper everything is legit and then our agreements are not bound by what the existing legal system requires that we do what is necessary in that realm. But the actual agreements are between us as peers." — Benjamin (\~21:11)

### **The Stewardship Council**

The Stewardship Council is **the operational mother circle of Spirit**. It is the sub-circle where strategy, vision, and inter-circle coordination happen. Its membership comprises:

- **Circle Leads** (one per circle, ex officio)  
- **Stewards** (a coordinating role distinct from circle lead)

Crucially, the team distinguished between three things that all use the word "steward":

"There's a difference between the act of stewardship, the responsibility of stewardship, and the role of steward." — Benjamin (\~21:15)

The **role of steward** is filled by individuals whose responsibility is *coordination across circles* and holding "the holistic strategy and direction of the organization":

"I think it's also reasonable to have a steward role that isn't a circle lead that's responsible for coordinating circle leads. And holding the kind of holistic strategy and direction of the organization." — Benjamin (\~21:13)

In practice, a single person may wear both hats — circle lead *and* steward — but the roles are distinct, and being a steward at the council level is its own designated responsibility (described later as "strategy, vision, protocols, and operations").

**Decision-making inside the council:** decisions are made by **consent** (sociocratic), not majority vote. Circle leads come to the council both as voices for their circles' needs *and* as participants in the broader strategy of Spirit.

### **Circle Leads**

Each of the five circles has a Circle Lead. The lead is responsible for:

- Defining the circle's purpose, responsibilities, and meeting cadence  
- Articulating the quarterly strategy and project list for the circle  
- Appointing project leads within the circle  
- Representing the circle inside the Stewardship Council

Circle leads are **appointed by stewards**, not elected:

"Board appoints stewards and stewards appoint circle leads... Circle leads appoint project leads. So we're doing appointments and not elections. For Spirit I think it's very appropriate to do that way." — Benjamin (\~21:21)

Why appointment, not election, *for Spirit*: Spirit is the institutional vehicle responsible for legal and fiduciary compliance. Appointment preserves accountability and avoids importing political dynamics into operational roles. The Commons, by contrast, *will* use election (see §3).

### **Project Leads & Team Structure (Lead / Support / Safety)**

Inside each circle, projects are led by Project Leads. Each project team carries three named functions:

- **Lead** — drives the project, schedules meetings, owns delivery  
- **Support** — assists the lead with execution, takes notes  
- **Safety** — independent check-and-balance role; ensures alignment with the integrity of the greater vision

"I do like the idea of each team having three. Like if it doesn't need three people, it's not a team... It should probably be at least three." — Cameron (\~21:23)

"\[Safety is\] kind of like the check and balance. Stop. Go. Making sure it's in alignment with the integrity of the like the greater vision." — group, on the safety role (\~21:29)

Three-person teams are the default, though some lightweight projects may be carried by a single contractor. The structure is intentional: it forces the explicit naming of accountability ("a shadow of my leadership is that I can just get really confused about whose responsibility is what, and then try to either take it all on or let things drop and just not understand who was supposed to pick up that ball" — Jordan, \~21:23).

## 2\. The Five Circles

The team reconciled two parallel inventories (one from the Notion workspace, one from the prior Women's Council meeting captured in a Google Doc) and confirmed that they were essentially the same five circles. Naming was tightened in the room.

"Oh my god, what if this feels easy? What if this could be easy?... We just reconciled the Notion and the Google Doc version list of prospective circles." — Kathleen (\~21:03)

The five circles:

### **2.1 Admin / Tech & Systems**

- **Purpose:** the operational and technical backbone of Spirit and the lattice infrastructure for the Commons.  
- **Scope:** internal systems, websites, tools, CRM, automation, technical platforms. Includes the Stewardship Council itself as a sub-circle ("Stewardship is actually the super circle that contains as sub-circles" — \~21:05).  
- **Provisional lead:** Cameron Ely-Murdock.

### **2.2 Culture, Community & Conflict**

- **Purpose:** holding the relational fabric — onboarding, gathering rhythms, conflict mediation, the cultural codex.  
- **Scope:** participation experience, the membership threshold (cultural codex as "a way of mediating a membership threshold, which has a governance implication"), conflict mediation processes, community care.  
- **Provisional lead:** Matthew Duffy.

### **2.3 Story, Media & Campaign**

- **Purpose:** the externally facing voice of Spirit — narrative, campaign work, media artifacts, identity.  
- **Scope:** "campaign and media... A.K.A. artifact, story, identity" (\~21:05). Pollination of the Spirit narrative into other bioregions is anticipated (treated as meta-work that may eventually move to the Commons or to a peer bioregion).  
- **Provisional lead:** Kathleen Rose.

### **2.4 Programming, Education & Events**

- **Purpose:** experiential offerings — workshops, gatherings, learning programs, events.  
- **Scope:** Solidarity Suppers, AI nights, Commons-teach-ins, the broader curriculum of "how the Commons works." Events expressly include "people who teach them how to do that and help them get engaged and participating with the system" (\~21:46).  
- **Provisional co-leads:** Sarah and Jordan.

### **2.5 Finance & Fundraising**

- **Purpose:** money in (development) and money out (disbursements, contributor pay).  
- **Scope:** quarterly budget cycle, contributor compensation, fiscal sponsorship/hosting agreements, grant administration to the Commons. Specific autonomy granted: **the Finance circle may pay pre-approved invoices autonomously**; unapproved invoices require full Stewardship Council approval (per the second session).  
- **Provisional lead:** Jordan ("Josie") Siegel — also serves as Culture co-lead in the interim.

**Note on selection:** the assignments above are *provisional*. The action item for May 1 is "each circle lead defines circle purpose, responsibilities, and Q1 project list." Re-confirmation of leads happens implicitly through that work — leads who do the work are leads.

## 3\. The Front Range Commons (Distinct Entity)

The deepest insight of the day was that **the Front Range Commons is not a department of Spirit.** It is a parallel, self-organizing entity that Spirit serves.

### **What the Commons Is**

The Commons is the bioregional social body — every commoner who has signed the membership agreement and committed to the cultural codex. It is *primarily relational*, not institutional.

"Culture is ultimately a question of the commons." — Naya (\~21:46)

"There's not really a lot of infrastructure that needs to be built for the commons because it already exists actually de facto. It's just figuring out how we articulate the thresholds for people to come into that, to create a repository for resources to come into there and then be distributed... That's actually soft stuff. That's not a hard thing." — Jordan (\~21:51)

### **Commons Governance: Wisdom Council and Common Stewards**

The Commons mirrors Spirit's tiered structure but with distinct authority and a **bottom-up** flow:

```
              ┌──────────────────────┐
              │     COMMONERS        │  ← members of the Commons
              └──────────┬───────────┘
                         │ elect
                         ▼
              ┌──────────────────────┐
              │   WISDOM COUNCIL     │  ← analog of the board
              │   (wisdom carriers)  │     advisory + accountability
              └──────────┬───────────┘
                         │ appoints / "champions"
                         ▼
              ┌──────────────────────┐
              │   COMMON STEWARDS    │  ← operational implementation
              └──────────────────────┘
```

"Wisdom Council appoints, advises the commons stewards, and the commoners elect the Wisdom Council. And the commons stewards effectuate the things that the commoners are part of. The common stewards run the ops of the commons as needed." — group consensus (\~22:04)

**The Wisdom Council** is the Commons' equivalent of a board. It is composed of *wisdom carriers* — explicitly framed as the people the broader team aspires for the Commons to be led by:

"Indigenous folks and elder folks. Like community leaders." — Jordan (\~21:17)

The Wisdom Council's role is not operational — it is advisory and direction-setting. It also provides accountability *to Spirit*: this is where the Commons holds Spirit responsible for the lattice work it is supposed to be doing.

"Stewardship for me is more about the active tending, which is more of an operational thing. Whereas advisement is more about wisdom and sensing, and being like, *you shouldn't do that.* Whereas to me stewardship is like, *has anyone checked on how that project is going?*" — Benjamin (\~21:42)

"We call it a Wisdom Council. Almost like at the level of the board on Spirit... So they would be kind of like the board of the Commons. And they would oversee the stewardship council of the commons, but they would also provide advisory support to Spirit." — group (\~21:42)

"It's made up of people that the Commons elects as wisdom carriers that provide accountability to the stewards of the Commons as well as to the stewards of Spirit." — Benjamin (\~21:42)

**Common Stewards** are the operational layer — they "effectuate" what the Wisdom Council points to. The metaphor that emerged in the room was striking:

"There's like a quality of where Wisdom Council is kind of being from a place of wisdom, from a place of some kind of elevated awareness, and that they pick who they want to take them to the ground... So the common stewards basically are the ones who are effectuating what the Wisdom Council wants. I really like them like paying basically almost like choosing their champions. The Common Stewards — the Wisdom Council's like, *yeah, please Sarah, take us home on this.*" — Naya (\~22:02)

**Compensation:** Common Stewards receive a stipend from Spirit. The principle was firm:

"I don't want anybody doing work that they're not getting paid for in this world. Unless they're so wealthy that they can meaningfully contribute. Or if they do it and they get paid and they contribute — I think that could be something they're like, *I'm happy to do this*, and we create \[the option\] if somebody is financially secure, that they donate their stipend into the \[grant\] round." — Jordan (\~22:03)

### **What the Commons Actually Does**

Operationally, the Commons is *light*. The team explicitly resisted recreating organizational mass at the Commons layer:

"The Commons is totally self-organizing. We put up money for some things, but..." — Benjamin (\~21:44)

"I imagine the stewardship of the Commons will mostly involve maintaining the calendar and the shared message board and moderating for racist comments from a weird old dude who gets access to the WhatsApp." — Benjamin (\~21:40)

"We don't want the Commons to balloon into another organization. Spirit is the organization that's doing the things that only organizations can do. And the Commons is doing stuff that people in relationships do organically already, and we're just enabling and supporting." — Benjamin (\~21:50)

What the Commons *does* hold:

- **Membership protocol** — onboarding new commoners, the cultural codex/ethics threshold  
- **Project intake & granting** — deciding which commoner-led projects get funded each quarter (via quadratic voting; see §5)  
- **Wisdom Council elections** — periodic  
- **Cultural / community / conflict work** — ultimately a Commons concern, not a Spirit concern

## 4\. The Lattice: Spirit ↔ Commons Relationship

This was the synthesis that crystallized the entire architecture. **Spirit is the lattice on which the Commons grows.**

"I like to think of Spirit as the lattice... So we should be enabling and making easier the things that already want to happen. And I think that should look like a minimum level of infrastructure." — Benjamin (\~21:48)

"Someone should be able to post something that they want to invite people to and it gets to the right people. And if we're the people managing that email list or we're providing the platform that people go and post that on — that's us enabling people finding out about activities that people in the community are already doing, as opposed to paying someone to go do that activity." — Benjamin (\~21:48)

The asymmetry is intentional:

- **Spirit** \= capital, fiduciary capacity, employment, technical infrastructure, formal partnerships, fiscal sponsorship — *the things that require an organization*.  
- **Commons** \= relationships, gatherings, projects, culture, mutual aid — *the things that organizations historically corrupt by trying to "manage."*

The team named the operating principle as **institutional submission** — the practice of voluntarily transferring responsibility from Spirit to the Commons as fast as the Commons can absorb it.

"I feel like you would have some clever name for this. It's like institutional submission or something... that we are trying to create the conditions by which we submit to our own creation. A little like Frankenstein's monster kind of thing." — Kathleen (\~21:52)

"I think the more of our responsibilities that we can offload to the Commons, probably the better." — group (\~21:47)

"I have a fear of it coming off — institution being too institutional with the ops. That's why I'm trying to down-regulate it operationally — like there should be no ops. There should be as little ops as possible." — Benjamin (\~22:07)

### **The Mutual Advisory Relationship**

Spirit's Stewardship Council and the Commons' Wisdom Council provide **mutual advisory accountability** to each other:

- The Wisdom Council holds Spirit accountable for *being a useful lattice* (not over-reaching, not under-providing).  
- The Spirit Stewardship Council provides operational advisory support to the Common Stewards (e.g., financial mechanics, legal questions, technical infrastructure).

"It is who the Commons elects as their stewardship council that we \[interface with\]." — Benjamin (\~21:41)

This is why the Wisdom Council is **independent of Spirit** — it is appointed by the Commons, not by Spirit's board. The structure prevents Spirit from quietly becoming the Commons' boss.

## 5\. Governance Process & Decision-Making

### **Decision Authority by Layer**

| Decision type | Authority |
| :---- | :---- |
| Bylaws, legal compliance, board officers | Board |
| Strategy, vision, inter-circle coordination, hiring decisions | Stewardship Council (consent) |
| Budget approvals, quarterly allocations to circles | Stewardship Council (consent) |
| Fiscal sponsorship / hosting agreements | Stewardship Council (full consensus) |
| Pre-approved invoice payment | Finance circle (autonomous) |
| Unapproved invoices | Stewardship Council |
| Public-facing communications on official channels | **Lazy consent** — stewards have a window to object before publication |
| Circle-scope operational decisions | Circle Lead |
| Project-scope operational decisions | Project Lead |
| Wisdom Council elections | Commoners |
| Common Steward appointments | Wisdom Council |
| Commons project grant allocation | Commoners (quadratic voting, with minimal QA gate) |

### **The Quarterly Cycle**

The second session formalized a **quarterly strategy-and-budget cycle** as the heartbeat of Spirit's operations:

1. Each circle lead submits a quarterly proposal (purpose, project list, budget request).  
2. The Stewardship Council reviews and approves quarterly budget allocations per circle.  
3. Funds flow down: circle → projects → contributors.  
4. The Commons receives its grant pool allocation as part of the same cycle.

A standardized **project proposal template** is owed by Cameron as a near-term deliverable.

### **Quadratic Voting for Commons Grants**

For Commons grant allocation, the team converged on **quadratic voting** — a mechanism that captures intensity of preference, not just direction:

"The way that quadratic voting works is you get voting credits and you can express *I really value this project*, but you have to spend more credits. And then at the end of it, it tallies up all the votes. And the percentage that your project receives is a percentage of the total votes that you've gotten." — Benjamin (\~22:00)

The only Spirit-side gate on Commons grants is a minimal QA check ("is this hate speech? — you want to keep that decision really light") to preserve the integrity of Spirit as a fiscal vehicle. Beyond that, the Commons decides what the Commons funds.

Long-term, the team anticipates this allocation logic moving onto a smart-contract-driven DAO (the "Commons fund distribution: quadratic voting, smart contract automation, escrow" line item from the secondary session). For now, it is operationalized manually.

### **Lazy Consent for Public Comms**

For outbound communications on official Spirit channels, the team adopted **lazy consent**: a draft is posted to the Stewardship Council with a defined objection window; absent objection, it publishes. This avoids slowing down responsive communication while preserving collective accountability.

### **Sociocratic Heritage**

The decision-making methodology draws on the **Working Together** framework (an anarchist community governance group out of Canada and the EU) that Benjamin worked with during the prior Regen Commons constitutional design effort. The "consent threshold matrix" from that body was on the table as the working reference.

"I worked with them for six months trying to design the constitution and government framework for the regen commons... It seems like a really solid enough framework and is much more oriented to mapping what's already here." — Benjamin (\~20:48)

## 6\. Financial Architecture

### **The Funding Cascade**

The team agreed a **tiered fundraising target** that orders priorities transparently:

| Tier | Amount (cumulative) | Use |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Tier 1** | \~$500,000 | Spirit operations: salaries, infrastructure, lattice provisions |
| **Tier 2** | \+\~$150,000 | Common Steward stipends |
| **Tier 3** | \+$500,000+ | Commons grant pool — disbursed via quadratic voting to commoner-proposed projects |

"We have a fundraising goal of $500,000 to cover all of our core expenses for a year. And then once we've reached that threshold, our next fundraising goal — the next $150K that we want to raise — goes into salaries for Common Stewards. And then the next $500k we raise on top of that goes into the grants program administered by the Commons, where any project that anyone in the Commons wants to do can apply for funding, and funding happens based on the community deciding what those priorities are." — Benjamin (\~21:49)

The cascade has a strategic side-effect: it *protects Spirit* from being a contested gatekeeper of grant decisions, and it positions Spirit as a **neutral pass-through funder** for the bioregion.

"I think pushing decision making to the edges of the community around what projects we fund as a kind of pass-through funder for the rest of the bioregion — it lets us remain... neutral, where we don't have to \[defend allocation choices\]. I've read so many grant applications and people get so upset when you deny them. So I think it actually gives us kind of an out and we can be like, *we gave $150,000 this quarter to the Commons. Go pitch your peers on why you think this project is worth being funded.*" — Benjamin (\~21:57)

### **Charity Watchdog Compliance**

A target **administrative overhead ratio of \<35%** is held as a baseline:

"Below 35% demonstrates sufficiency and trustworthiness to charity watchdogs and donors." — Benjamin (\~22:00)

"All the money we're paying to ourselves is to administer programs that are serving the community. I think that looks more like administrative overhead costs not exceeding 30%." — group (\~22:00)

The fundraising cascade is structured to keep Spirit on the right side of this ratio: salaries at Tier 1 are recovered as Tiers 2 and 3 push the program-to-overhead ratio favorable.

### **Contributor Compensation**

From the second session: a **proposed minimum hourly rate of $44/hr** for contributors. Josie owns the determination of the final rate (due July 31). The principle: contributors should not subsidize Spirit through unpaid labor.

A "basket" of hourly rates by role-type was discussed, with a **salary floor** for ongoing roles. Project leads and team members log hours; circle budgets fund those hours.

### **Project-vs-Organization Funding**

The second session also clarified the grant structure: applicants can apply either as a **project** (specific scope, specific budget) or as an **organization** (general operating support). This mirrors Boulder City grants conventions and gives commoner organizations a path to recurring funding without forcing every gathering to be re-pitched as a fresh project.

"Most regenerative projects are not one-and-done. So I almost wonder if there's a different way of conceiving how we're relating to the projects... we only fund projects that we can fund fully. So that we avoid having just like that little sort of like, *now I gotta keep keep* \[chasing them\]." — Naya (\~21:54)

### **Fiscal Sponsorship vs Partnership**

The team distinguished two distinct relationship types Spirit can hold with external groups:

- **Fiscal sponsorship** (Spirit hosts an entity's funds, takes legal responsibility) — requires **full Stewardship Council consensus** and a formal hosting agreement.  
- **Partnership** (collaboration without fiduciary entanglement) — handled at circle level, lighter agreement.

Ancestral Weavings was named as a candidate test case for partnership agreement.

## 7\. Membership & Participation: Badges and Hats

The team rejected formal hierarchical titles in favor of a **badges \+ hats** schema, which decouples competence/authorization from identity:

"I just think of it like when I was in Boy Scouts — you'd learn to tie the knot and then get the badge that says you can tie that knot." — group (\~21:33)

"You could also call it 'role,' but my resistance to 'role' is that people get egoically attached to roles. And if you're just like, *oh I have the badge that allows me to put that hat on*..." — group (\~21:33)

"I'm putting on my steward hat. We can also make hats." — group (\~21:34)

**Badges** describe what someone is qualified/authorized to do. **Hats** are the active role being worn in a given moment. Both are stored as Notion database records, with the database already configured with a "badges" object.

Operationally, this means a person holding multiple roles (e.g., circle lead \+ steward \+ project lead) doesn't need separate identities — they have multiple badges, and they explicitly put on the relevant hat for the relevant context.

Kathleen owns the design of the badging/hat system as a deliverable (ongoing, framework due TBD).

### **Commoner Definition**

A **commoner** is a member of the Front Range Commons who has signed the membership agreement and accepted the cultural codex/ethics threshold. Commoners:

- Vote in Wisdom Council elections  
- Submit and vote on Commons grant proposals (via quadratic voting)  
- Participate in commons-led programming and gatherings  
- Optionally hold badges that allow them to step into specific Spirit project roles when needed

The cultural codex is itself a governance instrument: it is the threshold and the maintained standard of participation.

## Appendix B — Glossary

| Term | Definition |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Spirit (of the Front Range)** | The 501(c)(3) institutional vehicle. Provides infrastructure, capital, employment, fiduciary capacity. Acts as the "lattice." |
| **Front Range Commons** | The bioregional social body — relational, self-organizing, distinct from Spirit. Holds culture, gatherings, projects, mutual aid. |
| **Board** | Spirit's legally required governing body. Perfunctory; appoints stewards. |
| **Stewardship Council** | Spirit's operational mother circle. Composed of stewards \+ circle leads. Decisions by consent. |
| **Steward (role)** | A council seat held by someone whose job is cross-circle coordination \+ holistic strategy. Distinct from circle lead, though one person may hold both. |
| **Circle Lead** | Lead of one of the five operating circles. Sits on Stewardship Council. Appointed by stewards. |
| **Project Lead** | Lead of a specific project inside a circle. Appointed by circle lead. |
| **Lead / Support / Safety** | The three named roles on every project team. Safety is an independent integrity check. |
| **Wisdom Council** | The Commons' governing body. Elected by commoners. Functions as the Commons' "board." Holds advisory power over both the Commons' stewards and Spirit's stewards. |
| **Common Stewards** | The Commons' operational layer. Appointed by Wisdom Council. Paid stipend by Spirit. Run day-to-day Commons ops. |
| **Commoner** | A member of the Front Range Commons. Has signed the membership agreement and accepted the cultural codex. |
| **Cultural Codex** | The ethical/practice threshold for Commons membership. Both governance instrument and cultural identity. |
| **Lattice** | Spirit's operating metaphor. The minimum infrastructure that enables Commons activity without controlling it. |
| **Institutional submission** | The deliberate practice of transferring responsibility from Spirit to the Commons as fast as the Commons can absorb it. |
| **Badges** | Authorizations / qualifications a person holds. |
| **Hats** | Active roles a person is currently wearing in a given moment. |
| **Lazy consent** | Decision protocol for public comms: drafts publish absent objection within a window. |
| **Quarterly cycle** | Spirit's heartbeat: circle leads propose → Stewardship Council allocates → funds cascade. |
| **Quadratic voting** | The Commons' grant allocation mechanism. Voters spend voting credits at quadratic cost to express intensity of preference. |
| **Pass-through funder** | Spirit's strategic posture: raise funds, allocate to Commons grant pool, remain neutral on programmatic content. |
