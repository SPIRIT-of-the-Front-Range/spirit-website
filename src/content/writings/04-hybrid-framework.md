Communities trying to organize around shared place face a hard problem: the kinds of institutions that can interface with grants, government, and the financial system tend to also concentrate authority — which is exactly what bioregional work is trying to move away from.

This paper offers a way through. It describes a *two-body* model — a small nonprofit that handles the legal scaffolding, paired with a larger community body that holds the actual work and the real authority. The framework draws on Elinor Ostrom's Nobel-winning research on how communities have successfully managed shared resources, and on practical experiments happening now.
